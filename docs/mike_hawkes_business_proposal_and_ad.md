# Mike Hawkes — Business Proposal and Advertisement Draft

## Working Business Name

**Mike Hawkes**

## Plain-English Positioning

**Mike Hawkes helps small local crews build and maintain the backend they need to run like a real business without hiring an IT department.**

The target customer is a 1–10 person local service business: contractor, repair company, landscaper, small shop, solo professional office, or local trade crew. These businesses do not need a full-time IT staff, a bloated managed service provider, or a complicated payment system on their website. They need the basic business plumbing to work.

They need customers to find them, contact them, request work, leave reviews, and trust that the business is real.

Mike Hawkes provides that backend.



# Business Proposal

## 1. Executive Summary

Small local businesses often lose money because their technical backend is duct-taped together. Their website is outdated or nonexistent. Their Google listing is wrong. Their email is personal Gmail. Their customer intake happens through texts, Facebook messages, voicemails, Angi leads, and random scraps of paper. Reviews go unanswered. Quote requests get missed. Passwords are shared. Devices break. Nobody owns the process.

Mike Hawkes exists to fix that problem.

The company provides practical technology, web, and workflow support for small local businesses that are too small to justify internal IT staff but too dependent on technology to keep winging it.

The core offer is simple:

**Own your customer pipeline. Stop renting it from lead platforms. Make it easy for customers to find you, contact you, request work, and leave reviews.**

## 3. Customer Problem

Small businesses do not usually have one huge technology problem. They have twenty annoying ones.

Examples:

- Customers cannot easily find the business online.
- The owner’s personal cell phone is the whole intake system.
- Quote requests are scattered across Facebook, texts, voicemail, email, and Angi.
- The business pays for leads instead of owning its own website and form.
- The Google Business Profile has wrong hours, wrong photos, or weak review handling.
- Bad reviews sit unanswered.
- The website is slow, broken, ugly, abandoned, or controlled by someone who disappeared.
- Employees share passwords.
- Email is not set up professionally.
- Nobody knows who owns the domain.
- A laptop, printer, phone, or router failure turns into a business interruption.
- There is no simple monthly report showing what customers searched, submitted, clicked, or complained about.

The business owner does not want a lecture. They want the mess handled.

## 4. Proposed Solution

Mike Hawkes provides a practical backend for small local businesses.

That backend can include:

- Website setup or cleanup
- Domain and DNS cleanup
- Business email setup
- Google Business Profile cleanup
- Review request links
- Review monitoring and response assistance
- Quote/request-work forms
- Intake spreadsheet or lightweight CRM setup
- Monthly lead/review/activity report
- Basic analytics
- Social media profile setup or cleanup
- Simple monthly posts using job photos, testimonials, and service reminders
- Device break/fix support
- Password/MFA/account recovery cleanup
- Vendor coordination
- Basic backup and file organization advice

The goal is not to overbuild.

The goal is to give the owner one clean path:

**Google search → business website → quote request → owner follow-up → review request → repeat.**

## 6. Important Boundaries

Mike Hawkes should avoid unnecessary liability by keeping early services simple and clean.

### Payment Boundary

Do not collect or store customer payment card data on client websites.

Use third-party hosted payment systems if payment links are needed later.

### Healthcare Boundary

Do not collect patient medical information or touch medical systems unless proper agreements, insurance, and compliance requirements are in place.

### Security Claims Boundary

Do not claim to make a business “secure,” “HIPAA compliant,” “ransomware-proof,” or “fully protected.”

Use specific language instead:

- “I can help clean up accounts.”
- “I can help enable MFA.”
- “I can help document what you have.”
- “I can help reduce obvious risk.”
- “I can help coordinate with your vendors.”

### Scope Boundary

Every engagement should say exactly what is included and what is not included.

Avoid open-ended “handle all IT forever” promises.
